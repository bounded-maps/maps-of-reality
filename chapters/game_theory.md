## The Optimal Strategy

Brutally simplifying things: if we view life as a game, the optimal strategy appears to be tit-for-tat from the iterated Prisoner's Dilemma.

This strategy follows four simple rules:
1. Be nice - start with cooperation (never defect first)
2. Be cooperative - default to collaborative behavior
3. Retaliate when wronged - immediately respond to defection by defecting once
4. Practice careful forgiveness - return to cooperation after retaliating, breaking cycles without becoming exploitable

I believe evolution may have favored humans who internalize tit-for-tat principles because they represent winning strategies for social survival. Without cooperation, proportional retaliation, and forgiveness, early human communication and socialization would have been difficult.

## Questions This Can Answer

**Life is not as simple as a simple PvP game or iterated Prisoner's Dilemma**

Yes, but I believe we can frame the environment to apply these principles or at least gain interpretation from this perspective. For example: Why cooperate in one-on-one situations? Because they positively reinforce the whole group to cooperate. "Laws" should fill the missing gaps, for example in retaliation (as punishment).

**Why unlawful acts like stealing or assault are bad**

In this case, treat the victims as the other players. Even though they may or may not retaliate, this will encourage violation of the first principle. As a society, we need to encourage people to follow these principles to maximize each person's gains.

**Do defectors have an optimal strategy in a tightly knit group with good people?**

First, laws exist to enforce these principles. For severe crimes or high-level defections, punishments make defection less favorable. What about minor crimes? See below.

- **Some of my simplified math for crimes, probabilities, and the gains**

  Calculating probabilities:
  1. You are not punished - prob: x, gain: a
  2. You are punished - prob: (1-x), gain: -b

  Ideally, expected gain should be less than 0:
  
  xa + (1-x)(-b) < 0
  
  b/a > x/(1-x)

  Basically, there should be stronger punishment than the odds of getting punished (Related Field: Deterrence Theory). It can't be this simple though, it doesnt make sense to give a strong punishment to minor offense.
